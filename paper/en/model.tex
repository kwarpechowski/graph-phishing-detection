\section{Model}
\label{sec:model}

violation. All quantities here are variables; the numbers that instantiate them are stated in the
Case Study (Sec.~\ref{sec:results}). Figure~\ref{fig:model} gives an overview.

\subsection{Domain knowledge graph}
\label{sec:dkg}
An organization's domain knowledge is relational in nature, encompassing who knows whom, who is responsible for what, and which
competencies, routines, or public footprints they have. It is modeled as a multiplex network, one set of
accounts with many types of relation stacked over it, each graph recording one kind of fact: who emails whom,
who shares a skill, who is active when. A real relationship appears on many of these graphs at once, while a forged or hijacked one appears on some but contradicts others. Detectors and the
susceptibility measure use that agreement or disagreement across the stack, so we first pin down
$\mathcal{G}$ and the two numbers that summarize a sender--victim pair, $C$ and $tc$.

\begin{definition}[Domain knowledge graph]
\label{def:dkg}
A domain knowledge graph is $\mathcal{G}=(V,\{E_L\}_{L\in\mathcal{L}},\{w_L\},\tau)$, where $V$ is the set of
accounts (people/roles), $\mathcal{L}$ is the set of relation \emph{layers} over the \emph{same} $V$ (contacts,
hierarchy, collaboration, and shared competencies/conferences/certificates, platforms, or routines; OSINT
footprints from the persona fields \texttt{c3}--\texttt{c8}, defined in Sec.~\ref{sec:results}), $w_L\in(0,1]$ is the confidence weight of a layer, and
$\tau:V\!\times\!V\!\to\!\mathcal{T}$ assigns to edges a \emph{temporal rhythm} (the sender's active windows).
Each layer encodes \emph{one type} of domain fact; superimposed, they form the entire knowledge of ``what is
normal''. The layers are grouped into three families: \emph{structural} layers (contacts, hierarchy, domain)
from the organizational chart, \emph{OSINT} layers (competencies, public footprint, platforms, events, routine,
pretexts) from publicly observable persona fields \texttt{c3}--\texttt{c8}, and the \emph{temporal} layer
(rhythm). The full eight-category persona schema (\texttt{c1}--\texttt{c8}) and its one-to-one mapping to
layers is given in the Case Study (Sec.~\ref{sec:cs-setup}, Tab.~\ref{tab:layers}); here we use only the
symbols.
\end{definition}

\noindent The definition is a stack of same-sized graphs over one set of people, each recording one
kind of fact. The two numbers below, $C$ and $tc$, summarize the agreement or disagreement across that
stack for a single sender--victim pair, and they are the only inputs the detectors and the susceptibility
measure use.

\noindent The property we exploit is the synergy between layers: a genuine relation is consistent across many of them at once, for example a known contact in its own rhythm with a real collaboration footprint, whereas a fabricated fact or a compromise breaks that consistency. This motivates two scalar summaries of a sender--victim pair. The
domain-knowledge coverage weights the layers in which the pair co-occurs,
\begin{equation}
C(s,v)\;=\;\sum_{L\in\mathcal{L}} w_L\,\mathbf{1}\!\left[(s,v)\in E_L\right],
\label{eq:consistency-model}
\end{equation}
and the temporal consistency tests whether the message arrives in the sender's rhythm,
$tc(s,v,t)=\mathbf{1}[t\in\tau(s)]$. A high $C$ together with $tc=0$ is the canonical signature of a compromise;
the two detection invariants below make this precise.

\subsection{Threat model: unifying lateral and external}
\label{sec:threat}
A single formalism covers both lateral/BEC and external impersonation, so one model and one detector family
suffice for both.

\smallskip The attacker's goal is to deliver to victim $v$ a message classified as legitimate. Two variants
follow the same formalism. In lateral/BEC the sender $s$ is a compromised internal account, a known contact of
$v$ writing outside its rhythm: coverage $C(s,v)$ is high yet $tc(s,v,t)=0$. In external impersonation, $s$
impersonates a role or authority, giving the impression of a high $C$ while lacking real coverage in some of the
layers (low structural consistency), possibly fabricating OSINT edges. In both cases the spear phisher respects the domain knowledge, which lends the content its credibility, but cannot reproduce it in full. This inconsistency is the signal.

\smallskip On capabilities and scope: on compromise the attacker gains mailbox access and so knows
the rhythm, making rhythm mimicry possible; fabricating OSINT footprints is cheap but does not create multilayer
consistency. Out of scope is an adaptive adversary with full knowledge of the graph and training poisoning. We
treat the signal as a cost increase for the attacker; no detection is final.

\subsection{Two detection invariants}
\label{sec:invariants}
The threat model says the attack must break consistency somewhere. It can break in two places, each mapping
to one attack mode, and their complementarity is the structural reason a single detector cannot cover
the targeted tail, a focus of the Case Study in
Sec.~\ref{sec:res-inv1} and~\ref{sec:res-inv2}.

\smallskip In the static case, a legitimate new sender sits outside the
contacts layer but on an OSINT layer, such as a shared list or affiliation, and that consistency justifies them.
A compromised account sits on the contacts layer but outside the rhythm, and that inconsistency betrays it. The
detector operates on the vector of layer membership and its (in)consistency, leaving content aside.

\smallskip In the temporal case, lateral phishing is a propagation process: a
compromised account sends to contacts, and from them to the next ones. A single message looks locally normal, but
the wave leaves a receiving-side signature, an atypical pattern of who in the victim's neighborhood was hit within
the window. A learned temporal model with node memory registers this pattern, scoring an event using the state from
before its bucket, without label leakage (made precise in Sec.~\ref{sec:method}).

\smallskip
The two invariants are complementary by attack mode: static inconsistency catches new senders and impersonation,
while dynamics catches compromises already in the graph. No single layer suffices; the signal is their fusion and
its (in)consistency.

\subsection{Organizational susceptibility}
\label{sec:m-susc}
Beyond detection, the graph exposes who is most at risk. Susceptibility depends on a node's position in the
graph, independent of any detector's tuning, and Sec.~\ref{sec:susceptibility} later measures it to answer
RQ3.

\begin{definition}[Susceptibility]
\label{def:susc}
The susceptibility of node $v$ is the expected \emph{detection uniqueness} of the best \emph{credible}
(domain-knowledge-respecting) attack targeting $v$,
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Susc}(v) \;=\; \max_{s\in \mathcal{A}(v)} \Big(1 - \Pr[\text{detector detects } (s\!\to\!v)]\Big),
\label{eq:susc-model}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{A}(v)$ is the set of \emph{plausible} impersonating senders of $v$ (high apparent coverage $C$,
e.g.\ an authority or a cross-departmental bridge).
\end{definition}

\noindent A node is susceptible when there exists a credible yet hard-to-detect attack against it; consequently
the definition takes the $\max$ over plausible senders and the $1-\Pr[\text{detect}]$ term. We do not enumerate
$\mathcal{A}(v)$ exhaustively; instead we correlate $\mathrm{Susc}(v)$ with graph-position features (authority
and centrality, number of cross-community bridges, rhythm regularity, OSINT coverage) and report which of them
drive it. The result is an organizational susceptibility profile that tells SOC teams which roles need
reinforced control, independently of the detector.

\subsection{Definition of detection effectiveness}
\label{sec:effectiveness}
Every result in the Case Study follows a standard that is detector-independent and operational; the
Methodology (Sec.~\ref{sec:method}) is the protocol that operationalizes it.

A high benchmark accuracy alone does not attest to detecting spear phishing, since a method works operationally for the targeted tail only when its advantage is causally relational-temporal,
vanishing under shuffle controls that destroy the relational or temporal signal, and transferable, so that the
ranking of methods established on one topology carries over to another. Effectiveness is judged by sensitivity
at a low false-positive rate with explicit base-rate accounting, rather than by headline AUC. This is what the leak-aware protocol of Sec.~\ref{sec:method}
implements.
