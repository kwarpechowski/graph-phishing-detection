\section{Model}
\label{sec:model}

This section states the formal model on which the rest of the paper rests: the domain knowledge graph, a threat
model that unifies lateral and external attacks, the two detection invariants, an organizational susceptibility
measure, and an operational definition of detection effectiveness. We keep it deliberately dry, using symbols and
relations only, with no concrete values. Every quantity introduced here is a variable; the numbers that
instantiate it (population size, number of layers, time resolution, weights) are deferred to the Case Study
(Sec.~\ref{sec:results}), so that the model reads as a specification and the Case Study as one realization of it.
Table~\ref{tab:notation} collects the symbols for reference, and Figure~\ref{fig:model} maps the model from
general to detailed: an organization induces a multiplex domain knowledge graph, whose two invariants feed two
detectors, which are judged by one definition of effectiveness and read off as susceptibility.

\subsection{Domain knowledge graph}
\label{sec:dkg}
An organization's domain knowledge is relational: who knows whom, who is responsible for what, which
competencies, routines, and public footprints they have. We model it as a multiplex network, one set of accounts
with many types of relation stacked over it. This subsection defines that object and the two derived quantities
that every later result is built on.

\smallskip\noindent\emph{Intuition before the formalism.} Read the definition below as a stack of same-sized
graphs over one shared set of people, where each graph records one kind of fact: who emails whom, who shares a
skill, who is active when. A real relationship appears on many of these graphs at once; a forged or hijacked one
appears on some but contradicts others. Everything that follows, both detectors and the susceptibility measure,
reads that agreement or disagreement across the stack. That is why we first pin down the stack $\mathcal{G}$ and
two numbers that summarize a single sender--victim pair, $C$ and $tc$.

\begin{definition}[Domain knowledge graph]
\label{def:dkg}
A domain knowledge graph is $\mathcal{G}=(V,\{E_L\}_{L\in\mathcal{L}},\{w_L\},\tau)$, where $V$ is the set of
accounts (people/roles), $\mathcal{L}$ is the set of relation \emph{layers} over the \emph{same} $V$ (contacts,
hierarchy, collaboration, shared competencies/conferences/certificates, platforms, routines --- OSINT
footprints from the persona fields \texttt{c3}--\texttt{c8}, defined in Sec.~\ref{sec:results}), $w_L\in(0,1]$ is the confidence weight of a layer, and
$\tau:V\!\times\!V\!\to\!\mathcal{T}$ assigns to edges a \emph{temporal rhythm} (the sender's active windows).
Each layer encodes \emph{one type} of domain fact; superimposed, they form the entire knowledge of ``what is
normal''.
\end{definition}

\noindent\emph{Why this object.} The property we exploit is the complementarity of layers. A genuine relation is
consistent across many of them at once (a known contact, in their own rhythm, with a real collaboration
footprint), whereas a fabricated fact or a compromise breaks that consistency. This motivates two scalar
summaries of a sender--victim pair. The domain-knowledge coverage weights the layers in which the pair co-occurs,
\begin{equation}
C(s,v)\;=\;\sum_{L\in\mathcal{L}} w_L\,\mathbf{1}\!\left[(s,v)\in E_L\right],
\label{eq:consistency-model}
\end{equation}
and the temporal consistency tests whether the message arrives in the sender's rhythm,
$tc(s,v,t)=\mathbf{1}[t\in\tau(s)]$. A high $C$ together with $tc=0$ is the canonical signature of a compromise;
the two detection invariants below make this precise.

\subsection{Threat model: unifying lateral and external}
\label{sec:threat}
Before the invariants, we fix what the attacker is trying to do and what it can do. A single formalism covers both
lateral/BEC and external impersonation, so one model and one detector family suffice for both.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Attacker goal.} Deliver to victim $v$ a message classified as legitimate. Two variants
follow the same formalism. In lateral/BEC the sender $s$ is a compromised internal account, a known contact of
$v$ writing outside its rhythm: coverage $C(s,v)$ is high yet $tc(s,v,t)=0$. In external impersonation, $s$
impersonates a role or authority, giving the impression of a high $C$ while lacking real coverage in part of the
layers (low structural consistency), possibly fabricating OSINT edges. In both cases the spear phisher respects
the domain knowledge, which lends the content its credibility, but cannot reproduce it in full, and that
inconsistency is the signal.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Capabilities and scope.} On compromise the attacker gains mailbox access and so knows
the rhythm, making rhythm mimicry possible; fabricating OSINT footprints is cheap but does not create multilayer
consistency. Out of scope is an adaptive adversary with full knowledge of the graph and training poisoning; we
treat the signal as a cost increase for the attacker, not an ultimate defense.

\subsection{Two detection invariants}
\label{sec:invariants}
The threat model says the attack must break consistency somewhere. The two invariants are the two places it can
break: across layers at a fixed time, and across time along a propagation path. They are complementary by attack
mode, a pairing the reader should carry into the results.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{(I) Cross-layer inconsistency (static).} A legitimate new sender sits outside the
contacts layer but on an OSINT layer, such as a shared list or affiliation, and that consistency justifies them.
A compromised account sits on the contacts layer but outside the rhythm, and that inconsistency betrays it. The
detector reads the vector of layer membership and its (in)consistency, not the content.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{(II) Cascade dynamics (temporal).} Lateral phishing is a propagation process: a
compromised account sends to contacts, and from them to the next ones. A single message looks locally normal, but
the wave leaves a receiving-side signature, an atypical pattern of who in the victim's neighborhood was hit within
the window. A learned temporal model with node memory reads this pattern, scoring an event using the state from
before its bucket, without label leakage (made precise in Sec.~\ref{sec:method}).

\smallskip
The two invariants are complementary by attack mode: static inconsistency catches new senders and impersonation,
while dynamics catches compromises already in the graph. No single layer suffices; the signal is their fusion and
its (in)consistency.

\subsection{Organizational susceptibility}
\label{sec:m-susc}
The graph does more than enable detection; it lets us measure exposure. We define the susceptibility of a node as
the vulnerability left by the best credible attack against it, so that susceptibility is a property of a position
in the graph, independent of any particular detector's tuning.

\begin{definition}[Susceptibility]
\label{def:susc}
The susceptibility of node $v$ is the expected \emph{detection uniqueness} of the best \emph{credible}
(domain-knowledge-respecting) attack targeting $v$,
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Susc}(v) \;=\; \max_{s\in \mathcal{A}(v)} \Big(1 - \Pr[\text{detector detects } (s\!\to\!v)]\Big),
\label{eq:susc-model}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{A}(v)$ is the set of \emph{plausible} impersonating senders of $v$ (high apparent coverage $C$,
e.g.\ an authority or a cross-departmental bridge).
\end{definition}

\noindent\emph{Proof sketch / reading.} A node is susceptible when there exists a credible yet hard-to-detect
attack against it, which is why the definition takes the $\max$ over plausible senders and the
$1-\Pr[\text{detect}]$ term. We do not enumerate $\mathcal{A}(v)$ exhaustively; instead we correlate
$\mathrm{Susc}(v)$ with graph-position features (authority and centrality, number of cross-community bridges,
rhythm regularity, OSINT coverage) and report which of them drive it. The result is an organizational
susceptibility profile that tells SOC teams which roles need reinforced control, independently of the detector.

\subsection{Definition of detection effectiveness}
\label{sec:effectiveness}
Finally we state what it means for a method to work here, since the usual ``high accuracy on a benchmark'' is the
claim we distrust. The definition below is the standard every result in the Case Study is held to; the protocol
that operationalizes it is the Methodology (Sec.~\ref{sec:method}).

A method works operationally for residual spear phishing if its advantage is (i)~causally relational-temporal, so
that it vanishes under shuffle controls that destroy the relational or temporal signal, and (ii)~transferable, so
that the ranking of methods established on one topology carries over to another. Effectiveness is judged by
sensitivity at a low false-positive rate with explicit base-rate accounting, not by headline AUC; $98\%$ on a
mass benchmark does not attest to detecting spear phishing. This detector-independent definition is itself a
contribution, and it is what the leak-aware protocol of Sec.~\ref{sec:method} enforces.
