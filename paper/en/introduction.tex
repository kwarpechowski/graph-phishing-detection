\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

A request lands in the CFO's inbox: the ``CEO'' asks for an urgent transfer. The wording is polished, the pretext plausible, and nothing in the content betrays the deception \citep{butavicius2022why,sturman2025security}. Such attacks are increasingly effective. FBI IC3 reports show that Business Email Compromise (BEC) caused adjusted losses of approximately \$2.9 billion in 2023 \citep{fbi2024ic3}. These often bypass content filters because the message itself is typically clean; the deception lies in the sender's identity and the communication context. This is spear phishing---the residual that mass detection fails to capture.

Phishing detection is often framed as an almost solved problem. Modern email filters, built on content and
reputation, classify mass campaigns at $\sim$98\% accuracy on typical benchmarks
\citep{birthriya2025detection,tan2020graph}. However, this figure is misleading. The remaining $2\%$---spear phishing and BEC---drives a large share of financial losses
\citep{cidon2019high,ho2019detecting} while largely evading content-based methods. This residual represents
the core of the problem.

The gap that lets this residual $2\%$ through is structural. A targeted attack
succeeds by respecting the victim's domain knowledge: roles, hierarchy, business relationships, and communication routines. The attacker assembles this knowledge from OSINT
\citep{dewan2014analyzing,brown2025web}, a process made cheaper by language models \citep{bethany2024evaluating}.
Maliciousness is thus determined by the sender and the context, while the wording remains incidental. The detection signal must
therefore lie beyond the content, in the structure of relationships and the dynamics of their violation.

Our thesis is that spear phishing is a violation of domain knowledge. We model this knowledge as a multiplex graph of relations: contacts and collaboration, supplemented by competencies, OSINT traces, and temporal rhythm. A targeted attack breaks this structure, manifesting through two invariants no content filter can see. First, cross-layer inconsistency: a genuine relation is typically consistent across many layers, whereas impersonation appears in one but contradicts others---for example, a known name arriving off-rhythm or without an OSINT trace. Second, propagation dynamics. Lateral phishing spreads as a cascade; a compromised account sending to its contacts off-rhythm leaves a wave-like signature on the receiving side that no single message reveals. These invariants cover both primary modes of targeted attack: lateral/BEC from a compromised internal account, and external impersonation of a role or authority.

We make the thesis testable by splitting it into three research
questions, each paired with a measurable sub-thesis.
\begin{description}[leftmargin=1.4em,noitemsep,topsep=2pt]
  \item[RQ1 (static signal).] Does cross-layer inconsistency separate targeted attacks from legitimate new
  senders? Sub-thesis~T1: a multiplex of domain-knowledge layers raises sensitivity@FPR over a contacts-only
  graph and removes new-sender false alarms, and the gain vanishes under a layer/rhythm shuffle control.
  \item[RQ2 (temporal signal).] Can a compromised account, consistent across all static layers, be detected from
  cascade dynamics, and does the signal survive a stealthy attacker? Sub-thesis~T2: a temporal detector reading
  the receiving-side wave beats volume detection at low FPR and does not break down under a stealthy attacker; the
  advantage vanishes under a time-shuffle control and transfers to real topologies.
  \item[RQ3 (measurement and limits).] Which organizational roles and relations are most exposed, and what are the
  operational limits of the signal? Sub-thesis~T3: susceptibility is governed by relation rather than rank, and at
  a realistic base rate the detectors serve as a triage instrument rather than an autonomous defense.
\end{description}
\noindent The aim is to show that the two invariants carry a causal, transferable signal and to mark where it
stops being operationally useful. The learning models are standard tools throughout.

To ensure measurement rigor, we adopt a methodology that avoids a common pitfall: graph-based detectors are
easy to inflate. Real-world anchors often leak rhythm, degree, or community alignment with labels, while
synthetic generators can favor certain model classes. We follow the caution of
\citet{arp2022dos} and the TESSERACT protocol \citep{pendlebury2019tesseract} by adopting a leak-aware
evaluation and a predictivity probe. This approach tests whether the ranking of methods transfers across
topologies, establishing a standard for what effectiveness means for the targeted tail. The learning models we employ---a temporal graph network and gradient boosting---serve as measurement
tools to quantify the two invariants; the paper's contribution is a way of framing and measuring targeted
attacks, not a new machine-learning architecture.

Our contributions map onto the research questions as follows.
\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep,topsep=2pt]
  \item The notion and formalization of the domain knowledge graph, and the thesis that spear phishing is its
  violation, uniformly for lateral/BEC and external impersonation (the Model, Section~\ref{sec:model}); the
  foundation for RQ1--RQ3.
  \item Two detection invariants (cross-layer inconsistency, cascade dynamics) that beat strong baselines under a
  content-evasion regime, with causal controls (RQ1, RQ2; T1, T2).
  \item An organizational susceptibility metric that measures which roles, relations, and communication patterns
  are most exposed (RQ3; T3, Section~\ref{sec:susceptibility}).
  \item A leak-aware evaluation methodology and an operational definition of detection effectiveness, with the
  limits stated explicitly: FPR, base rate, and adversarial cost (RQ3; T3).
\end{enumerate}
