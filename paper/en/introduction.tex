\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Phishing detection is often presented as an almost solved problem. Modern email filters, built on content
(lexical features, URLs, attachments) and reputation, classify mass campaigns with high accuracy, on the order of
$\sim$98\% on typical benchmarks \citep{birthriya2025detection,tan2020graph}. That number is misleading. The
remaining traffic, spear phishing and business email compromise (BEC), accounts for a large share of financial
losses \citep{cidon2019high,ho2019detecting} yet is almost invisible to content-based methods. This residual
$2\%$ is the real problem.

\paragraph{Why the residual $2\%$ gets through.} The gap is not accidental or transient; it is a structural
property of the targeted attack. Spear phishing works because it respects the victim's domain knowledge: roles
and hierarchy, business relationships, competencies, communication routines, and project context. A request from
the ``CEO'' to the CFO for an urgent transfer is, in its content, indistinguishable from a legitimate one, and
plausible in light of what is known about the organization \citep{butavicius2022why,sturman2025security}. The
attacker assembles that knowledge from OSINT \citep{dewan2014analyzing,brown2025web}, and language models lower
its cost \citep{bethany2024evaluating}. Because maliciousness is fixed by who sent a message and in what context
rather than by its wording, the detection signal must lie beyond the content, in the structure of relationships
and in the dynamics of their violation.

\paragraph{Thesis: spear phishing violates domain knowledge.} We model an organization's domain knowledge as a
multiplex graph of relations over a set of accounts (contacts, collaboration, competencies, OSINT traces,
temporal rhythm). In this framing a targeted attack shows up through two invariants that a content filter cannot
see. The first is cross-layer inconsistency: a genuine relation is consistent across many layers at once, while
impersonation (external or a compromised account) appears in one layer but contradicts others, for example a
known name that arrives off-rhythm or without any OSINT trace. The second is propagation dynamics: lateral
phishing spreads as a cascade, so a compromised account sending to its contacts off its own rhythm leaves a
wave-like signature on the receiving side that a single message does not reveal. One framing thus covers both
modes of targeted attack, lateral/BEC (a compromised internal account) and external impersonation of a role or
authority.

\paragraph{Research questions and sub-theses.} We make the thesis testable by splitting it into three research
questions, each paired with a measurable sub-thesis.
\begin{description}[leftmargin=1.4em,noitemsep,topsep=2pt]
  \item[RQ1 (static signal).] Does cross-layer inconsistency separate targeted attacks from legitimate new
  senders? Sub-thesis~T1: a multiplex of domain-knowledge layers raises sensitivity@FPR over a contacts-only
  graph and removes new-sender false alarms, and the gain vanishes under a layer/rhythm shuffle control.
  \item[RQ2 (temporal signal).] Can a compromised account, consistent across all static layers, be detected from
  cascade dynamics, and does the signal survive a stealthy attacker? Sub-thesis~T2: a temporal detector reading
  the receiving-side wave beats volume detection at low FPR and does not break down under a stealthy attacker; the
  advantage vanishes under a time-shuffle control and transfers to real topologies.
  \item[RQ3 (measurement and limits).] Which organizational roles and relations are most exposed, and what are the
  operational limits of the signal? Sub-thesis~T3: susceptibility is governed by relation rather than rank, and at
  a realistic base rate the detectors serve as a triage instrument rather than an autonomous defense.
\end{description}
\noindent The aim is not a deployable filter or a new learning architecture, but to show that the two invariants
carry a causal, transferable signal and to mark where it stops being operationally useful. The learning models
are off-the-shelf instruments throughout.

\paragraph{Measurement rigor.} A central concern of this work is methodology. Graph-based detectors are easy to
inflate: real-world anchors leak rhythm, degree, and community alignment with labels, and synthetic generators
favor certain model classes. Following the caution of \citet{arp2022dos} and the TESSERACT protocol
\citep{pendlebury2019tesseract}, we adopt a leak-aware evaluation and a predictivity probe that tests whether the
ranking of methods transfers across topologies, and we use that same standard to define what effectiveness means
for residual spear phishing. The learning models we employ, a temporal graph network and gradient boosting, are
measurement instruments that quantify the two invariants; the contribution is conceptual and methodological, a
way of framing and measuring targeted attacks, not a new machine-learning architecture.

\paragraph{Contributions.} The contributions map onto the research questions as follows.
\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep,topsep=2pt]
  \item The notion and formalization of the domain knowledge graph, and the thesis that spear phishing is its
  violation, uniformly for lateral/BEC and external impersonation (the Model, Section~\ref{sec:model}); the
  foundation for RQ1--RQ3.
  \item Two detection invariants (cross-layer inconsistency, cascade dynamics) that beat strong baselines under a
  content-evasion regime, with causal controls (RQ1, RQ2; T1, T2).
  \item An organizational susceptibility metric that measures which roles, relations, and communication patterns
  are most exposed (RQ3; T3, Section~\ref{sec:susceptibility}).
  \item A leak-aware evaluation methodology and an operational definition of detection effectiveness, with the
  limits stated explicitly: FPR, base rate, and adversarial cost (RQ3; T3).
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Organization.} Section~\ref{sec:related} reviews spear phishing, human susceptibility, graph-based
approaches, and evaluation methodology. Section~\ref{sec:model} formalizes the domain knowledge graph, the threat
model, the two invariants, and the definition of effectiveness; Section~\ref{sec:method} gives the verification
procedure and the leak-aware protocol. Section~\ref{sec:results} instantiates the model in a case study and
reports the detection results, the susceptibility measurement, and the rigor analysis, followed by a discussion
of the limits and practical implications.
