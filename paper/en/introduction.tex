\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

A request lands in the CFO's inbox: the ``CEO'' asks for an urgent transfer. The wording is polished, the
pretext plausible, and nothing in the content betrays the deception \citep{butavicius2022why,sturman2025security}. A content
filter has no reason to stop it, because there is nothing wrong with the message --- only with who sent it, and
in what context. This is spear phishing, and it is the residual that mass detection never reaches.

Phishing detection is often presented as an almost solved problem. Modern email filters, built on content and
reputation, classify mass campaigns at $\sim$98\% accuracy on typical benchmarks
\citep{birthriya2025detection,tan2020graph}. That number is misleading, as the residual --- spear phishing and BEC --- drives a large share of financial losses
\citep{cidon2019high,ho2019detecting} while largely evading content-based methods; this residual $2\%$ represents
the core of the problem.

The gap that lets this residual $2\%$ through is structural rather than transient, as a targeted attack
succeeds by respecting the victim's domain knowledge: roles and hierarchy, business relationships, competencies,
communication routines, project context. The attacker assembles that knowledge from OSINT
\citep{dewan2014analyzing,brown2025web}, and language models lower its cost \citep{bethany2024evaluating}.
Maliciousness is determined by who sent a message and in what context, and the wording is incidental. The detection signal must
therefore lie beyond the content, in the structure of relationships and the dynamics of their violation.

Our thesis is that spear phishing is a violation of domain knowledge. An organization's domain knowledge can be modeled
as a multiplex graph of relations over its accounts: contacts, collaboration, competencies, OSINT traces,
temporal rhythm. A targeted attack breaks this structure. It manifests through two invariants no content filter
can see. Cross-layer inconsistency is the first: a genuine relation is consistent across many layers at once,
whereas impersonation --- external or a compromised account --- appears in one layer but contradicts the others,
such as a known name that arrives off-rhythm or without any OSINT trace. Then come propagation dynamics.
Lateral phishing spreads as a cascade; a compromised account sending to its contacts off-rhythm leaves
a wave-like signature on the receiving side that no single message reveals. Together, these invariants cover
both modes of targeted attack: lateral/BEC from a compromised internal account, and external impersonation
of a role or authority.

We make the thesis testable by splitting it into three research
questions, each paired with a measurable sub-thesis.
\begin{description}[leftmargin=1.4em,noitemsep,topsep=2pt]
  \item[RQ1 (static signal).] Does cross-layer inconsistency separate targeted attacks from legitimate new
  senders? Sub-thesis~T1: a multiplex of domain-knowledge layers raises sensitivity@FPR over a contacts-only
  graph and removes new-sender false alarms, and the gain vanishes under a layer/rhythm shuffle control.
  \item[RQ2 (temporal signal).] Can a compromised account, consistent across all static layers, be detected from
  cascade dynamics, and does the signal survive a stealthy attacker? Sub-thesis~T2: a temporal detector reading
  the receiving-side wave beats volume detection at low FPR and does not break down under a stealthy attacker; the
  advantage vanishes under a time-shuffle control and transfers to real topologies.
  \item[RQ3 (measurement and limits).] Which organizational roles and relations are most exposed, and what are the
  operational limits of the signal? Sub-thesis~T3: susceptibility is governed by relation rather than rank, and at
  a realistic base rate the detectors serve as a triage instrument rather than an autonomous defense.
\end{description}
\noindent The aim is to show that the two invariants carry a causal, transferable signal and to mark where it
stops being operationally useful. The
learning models are off-the-shelf instruments throughout.

To ensure measurement rigor, we focus on methodology to avoid a common pitfall: graph-based detectors are
easy to inflate. Real-world anchors often leak rhythm, degree, and community alignment with labels, and
synthetic generators can favor certain model classes. To counter this, we follow the caution of
\citet{arp2022dos} and the TESSERACT protocol \citep{pendlebury2019tesseract}, adopting a leak-aware
evaluation and a predictivity probe. This approach tests whether the ranking of methods transfers across
topologies, establishing a strict standard for what effectiveness means for the targeted tail. The
learning models we employ, a temporal graph network and gradient boosting, serve as measurement
instruments to quantify the two invariants; the paper's contribution is a way of framing and measuring targeted
attacks, not a new machine-learning architecture.

Our contributions map onto the research questions as follows.
\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep,topsep=2pt]
  \item The notion and formalization of the domain knowledge graph, and the thesis that spear phishing is its
  violation, uniformly for lateral/BEC and external impersonation (the Model, Section~\ref{sec:model}); the
  foundation for RQ1--RQ3.
  \item Two detection invariants (cross-layer inconsistency, cascade dynamics) that beat strong baselines under a
  content-evasion regime, with causal controls (RQ1, RQ2; T1, T2).
  \item An organizational susceptibility metric that measures which roles, relations, and communication patterns
  are most exposed (RQ3; T3, Section~\ref{sec:susceptibility}).
  \item A leak-aware evaluation methodology and an operational definition of detection effectiveness, with the
  limits stated explicitly: FPR, base rate, and adversarial cost (RQ3; T3).
\end{enumerate}

(Sec.~\ref{sec:model}), methodology (Sec.~\ref{sec:method}), and the case study with results
