\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusion}

Modern phishing detection works in aggregate, yet a residual fraction, spear phishing and BEC, remains the
most costly and largely evades content filters. The gap is structural, and by modeling an organization's domain
knowledge as a graph, the resulting inconsistency can be measured.

We answer the three research questions in turn.
\begin{description}[noitemsep,topsep=2pt]
  \item[RQ1 (static signal, T1):] The cross-layer inconsistency invariant outperforms a single
  contact graph by removing false alarms from legitimate new senders (Section~\ref{sec:res-inv1}), provided there
  is a shared, observable domain-knowledge edge; this gain vanishes under the layer/rhythm shuffle control.
  \item[RQ2 (temporal signal, T2):] The temporal invariant addresses the insensitivity of the static layers and,
  unlike a volume-based detector, maintains performance against a stealthy attacker (Section~\ref{sec:res-inv2}); this
  advantage is causally temporal and transfers to three real graphs.
  \item[RQ3 (measurement, T3):] Results indicate that susceptibility is driven more by the communication channel than by seniority. Cold external impersonation
  results in a margin of $\mathrm{Susc}\!\approx\!0.96$ at every level, whereas in the lateral channel the
  periphery is most exposed, rather than the executives (Section~\ref{sec:susceptibility}). At a realistic base rate
  the detectors act as triage instruments, with effectiveness defined operationally through low-FPR sensitivity
  under leak controls and a predictivity probe (Section~\ref{sec:res-rigor}).
\end{description}

As its contributions, the paper offers the concept and formalization of the domain knowledge graph,
with the thesis that spear phishing is its violation, uniformly for lateral/BEC and external impersonation.
On that foundation stand two complementary detection invariants, cross-layer inconsistency and cascade
dynamics, with causal controls and validation on three real communication graphs. A measured organizational
susceptibility metric shows the residual margin is governed by relation, and that in the lateral channel the
periphery is more exposed than the top. Wrapping these are a leak-aware evaluation methodology and an
operational definition of spear-phishing detection effectiveness.

Among the limitations, the maliciousness is synthetic, so we validate the mechanism rather than the
detection of real intrusions; the susceptibility measurement inherits the assumptions of the event model; and
scale and the operational threshold remain limitations. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} gives the details.

Taken together, the results turn targeted email into a quantity that can be tracked rather than merely flagged: a
receiving-side cascade signal holds at AUC $0.90$ against a stealthy attacker where volume detection drops
below random, cold impersonation leaves a structural susceptibility floor of $\mathrm{Susc}\!\approx\!0.96$,
and the largest lateral margin falls on the periphery rather than the executives. The defense this points toward, which is the broader implication of the work, asks
not whether a message looks malicious, but whether it fits what the organization knows about itself. Turning that question into an operational filter, at the precision real deployment demands, remains an open problem.
