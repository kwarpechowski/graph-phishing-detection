\section{Case Study and Results}
\label{sec:results}

This section is the one place where the abstract model of Sec.~\ref{sec:model} takes concrete values. We first
describe the \emph{case study} --- the organizational population, the eight-category persona schema, and the
multiplex layers and cascade parameters that instantiate $\mathcal{G}$ --- and then report the results, so that
every number below can be traced back to a symbol defined earlier.

% ============================================================
\subsection{Case study: population and graph instantiation}
\label{sec:cs-setup}
We realize the domain knowledge graph $\mathcal{G}$ (Def.~\ref{def:dkg}) on a population of $N{=}150$ synthetic
personas generated by a \emph{graph-first} method: an organization graph is designed deterministically
($19$~companies of $\sim\!8$~people each, with a supervisor--subordinate hierarchy and business partners between
companies), and each persona is then generated \emph{in its node}, so that its relations (supervisor, coworkers,
external contacts) are \emph{real other personas} --- the graph closes internally. The size $N{=}150$ matches the
number of core users of the Enron corpus \citep{klimt2004enron}, enabling external validation of the synthetic
graph against the real one at the same scale.

\paragraph{Persona schema (the eight categories \texttt{c1}--\texttt{c8}).} Each persona is a record with a
fixed eight-category schema. These categories are exactly the OSINT material available to the attacker ---
referenced abstractly as \texttt{c3}--\texttt{c8} in Def.~\ref{def:dkg} --- and each maps to at most one graph
layer, so that every layer in Tab.~\ref{tab:layers} has a single, named source category.
\begin{description}[leftmargin=2.6em,style=nextline,noitemsep,topsep=2pt]
  \item[\texttt{c1}] \textbf{Professional identity}: company, role, tenure --- fixes the account in the hierarchy.
  \item[\texttt{c2}] \textbf{Relationships}: supervisor, coworkers, external contacts --- the source of the contacts layer.
  \item[\texttt{c3}] \textbf{Competencies}: skills and certifications --- source of the \emph{skills} and \emph{certificates} OSINT layers.
  \item[\texttt{c4}] \textbf{Public footprint}: interests, OSS, conferences, publications --- source of the \emph{interests} and \emph{conferences} layers.
  \item[\texttt{c5}] \textbf{Contact channels}: platforms and handles --- source of the \emph{platforms} layer.
  \item[\texttt{c6}] \textbf{Temporal context}: upcoming events and activity windows --- source of the \emph{events} layer and the rhythm $\tau$.
  \item[\texttt{c7}] \textbf{Persuasion profile}: authority, scarcity, social proof (per Cialdini) --- not a graph layer; used to parameterize attack plausibility.
  \item[\texttt{c8}] \textbf{Threat model}: routine communications and plausible pretexts --- source of the \emph{routine} and \emph{pretexts} layers.
\end{description}
\noindent The graph layers (Tab.~\ref{tab:layers}) are derived from \texttt{c3}--\texttt{c8} deterministically;
\texttt{c1}--\texttt{c2} give the structural layers and \texttt{c7} feeds the attack model rather than a layer.
Table~\ref{tab:fielddist} reports how densely these fields are populated, confirming that the derived OSINT
layers are well supported.

\noindent\emph{What the two columns mean.} Both describe the same field but from complementary angles.
\textbf{Avg.\ per person} is the mean count of distinct (non-generic) values a persona declares in that field; it
is the \emph{node side} of the layer --- a persona's out-degree potential, i.e.\ how many shared-value edges it
can form. \textbf{Unique values} is the cardinality of the field's global vocabulary across all $150$~personas;
it is the \emph{population side} --- a large vocabulary spread over few people yields \emph{specific, sparse}
edges (a shared value is meaningful), whereas a small vocabulary yields \emph{dense, generic} edges. The ratio of
the two therefore predicts a layer's edge density: many values per person over a small vocabulary (e.g.\
\texttt{c5.platforms}: $2.5$ over $29$) gives a dense layer, while few values over a large vocabulary (e.g.\
\texttt{c8.plausible\_pretexts}: $3.7$ over $474$) gives a sparse, discriminative one.

\smallskip\noindent\emph{Reading one row.} Take the first row, \texttt{c3.skills}. ``avg.\ per person $=4.5$'' is
the mean number of distinct skills a persona declares (e.g.\ ``incident response, SIEM, Python, \dots''); this is
the fan-out that determines how many others a persona can share a skill with. ``unique values $=271$'' is the
size of the global skill vocabulary: with $271$ distinct skills spread over $150$~people at $4.5$ each, overlaps
are frequent but not trivial, which is exactly what makes a shared-skill edge (Tab.~\ref{tab:layers}) informative
rather than degenerate. The remaining rows read the same way, and since every field is filled for every persona,
the derived OSINT layers all cover the whole population.

\paragraph{Multiplex layers (Invariant~I).} Each layer is a separate graph over the same $150$~nodes
(Fig.~\ref{fig:realgraph}). The \emph{structural} layers (contacts, hierarchy, domain) come from the organization
graph; the \emph{OSINT} layers are built from fields \texttt{c3}--\texttt{c8} --- two personas share an edge in a
layer when they share a concrete, publicly observable field value (co-occurrence). Generic values (e.g.\
``LinkedIn'') are filtered out, and fields yielding a degenerate (near-complete) or too-sparse layer are omitted
--- a realistic data limitation, not a designed convenience. The only adversarial knob is the \emph{fabrication
rate} (how many OSINT edges the attacker injects), with which we study robustness. Table~\ref{tab:layers} gives
the edge semantics, confidence weight $w_L$, and edge count of each layer.

\paragraph{Temporal layer and cascade parameters (Invariant~II).} We divide the week into $T{=}28$ buckets
(7~days $\times$ 4~six-hour blocks); an account's rhythm $\tau$ is the set of its active buckets ($6$ per persona
synthetically; on Enron derived from the \texttt{Date} headers). Realistic noise: $15\%$ of legitimate mail
arrives off-rhythm and $25\%$ of attacks mimic the rhythm. For the cascade (Sec.~\ref{sec:m-detectors}) the
per-contact infection probability is $p_\mathrm{inf}{=}0.6$ and the maximum depth is $H{=}4$. Detector
hyperparameters are fixed uniformly (Tab.~\ref{tab:hparams}).

\paragraph{Attack scenarios and simulation budget.} Beyond the default cascade we probe four \emph{extreme}
scenarios, each chosen to stress a specific claim rather than to represent typical traffic. \emph{(i)~Naive
blast}: one carrier infects all contacts at once --- the easy case that \emph{volume} detection should win,
included as a sanity ceiling. \emph{(ii)~Stealthy spread} ($K$~carriers, one send per interval, down to $K{=}1$):
the burst is smeared out, so this is the case that \emph{breaks} volume detection and isolates the
receiving-side signal. \emph{(iii)~Single-recipient surgical attack} ($p_\mathrm{inf}{=}0$, fan-out~$1$): no
propagation at all --- the \emph{blind spot} that no cascade detector can see, included to mark the lower
boundary of the threat model. \emph{(iv)~Fabrication sweep} (attacker injects up to $50\%$ false OSINT edges):
stresses Invariant~I under forged domain knowledge. Every headline contrast is averaged over $\ge\!20$ random
seeds on synthetic data; real-graph validations use $8$/$5$/$3$~seeds (lower variance on a fixed structure), with
a Wilcoxon test where significance is decisive. These scenarios and their purposes recur, by name, throughout the
results below.

\subsection{Results overview}
\label{sec:res-overview}
We organize the results around three claims and their rigor. Invariant~I (\ref{sec:res-inv1}), cross-layer
inconsistency, detects a new sender or impersonation. Invariant~II (\ref{sec:res-inv2}), cascade dynamics,
detects a compromised account that is consistent across all static layers and does not break down against a
stealthy attacker. The susceptibility measurement (\ref{sec:susceptibility}) uses these detectors as an
instrument. Real-graph validation (\ref{sec:res-realgraphs}) and fusion with content (\ref{sec:res-content}) test
transfer and limits, and the rigor analysis (\ref{sec:res-rigor}) determines what the numbers mean. We report
every result as AUC and sensitivity@FPR$1\%$, on a victim/organization split, over $\ge\!20$~seeds, with a causal
shuffle control.

% ============================================================
\subsection{Invariant~I: cross-layer inconsistency}
\label{sec:res-inv1}
This subsection tests sub-thesis~T1 (RQ1): whether fusing the domain-knowledge layers separates targeted attacks
from legitimate new senders better than a plain contacts graph, and whether that gain reflects real cross-layer
structure rather than added feature capacity.

\paragraph{Multiplex vs single graph.} We compare the contacts-only detector with the static multiplex (contacts
$+$ eight OSINT layers from real fields). In Tab.~\ref{tab:inv1} the multiplex beats the single graph (AUC $0.87$
vs $0.70$), and the shuffle control drops to random ($0.51$), so the advantage comes from real cross-layer
structure rather than feature capacity. The difference shows most in the false alarms: the contacts detector
flags $100\%$ of legitimate new senders as attacks, the multiplex $0\%$, because OSINT coverage rescues a
legitimate sender who shares an observable fact with the victim. The claim is conditional: we distinguish a
legitimate new sender from an attack when the sender shares a real domain-knowledge edge with the victim, while a
cold sender remains irreducibly confusing (\ref{sec:susceptibility}). At $50\%$ OSINT edge fabrication the
advantage degrades toward the control ($0.87\!\to\!0.74$), as false traces erode the signal.

\paragraph{The signal is consistency, not feature capacity.} To show this, we define four explicit consistency
measures (OSINT coverage; known sender off-rhythm; new sender on OSINT; entirely off-graph) and compare them with
raw feature concatenation (Tab.~\ref{tab:consistency}). Under logistic regression the explicit operator improves
AUC ($0.840\!\to\!0.860$), because the linear model cannot learn the conjunction on its own; under trees the
difference vanishes ($0.858$ vs $0.860$). The four explicit measures almost match the full concatenation, so the
signal is concise consistency, and its value is explainability and deployability (a single query) rather than
higher recall.

\paragraph{Layer ablation and selection.} Cumulative ablation (Fig.~\ref{fig:ablation}) shows that each OSINT
layer adds a little (contacts $0.57\!\to$ static $0.65$), but the temporal consistency layer dominates: the full
model reaches $0.86$, and removing that layer sets AUC back to $0.65$. Forward selection and leave-one-out on the
full set of $12$~layers (Fig.~\ref{fig:layersel}) confirm it. Temporal consistency on its own reaches $0.826$ out
of the full model's $0.862$ (leave-one-out $0.21$, by far the largest), subsequent OSINT layers add $\sim\!0.005$
each, and the hierarchy, domain, and co-activity layers have leave-one-out $\le0.002$, marking their redundancy.
The minimal sufficient set is temporal consistency plus three or four OSINT layers.

\paragraph{Layer redundancy is a topology artifact, not a property of the method.} The high correlation of the
structural layers (contacts$\approx$hierarchy$\approx$domain, Jaccard $\ge0.9$) stems from the generator, which
gives one domain per company. Introducing a topology-decorrelation knob $\rho$ that rewires contacts from intra-
to inter-company (Tab.~\ref{tab:topo}), we find that already at moderate decorrelation Jaccard drops to $0.39$
and the independent contribution of the structural layers rises to $0.25$ AUC. The ``effectively 3--4 layers''
figure is thus a property of our compact population, not of the multiplex as a method.

\paragraph{Layers are complementary by threat.} Time dominance in the aggregate masks the structure. Separating
the task by threat (Fig.~\ref{fig:bythreat}) shows the complementarity directly: for new senders the signal is
carried by OSINT (alone $0.77$) and time is useless ($0.50$), and for compromises the reverse holds (time $0.87$,
OSINT $0.50$). Each group is indispensable for its own failure mode. The sensitivity analysis
(Fig.~\ref{fig:sensitivity}) confirms that the ranking (full $>$ contacts $>$ static) holds across the whole
noise range and for every $N\in\{40,\dots,150\}$, though the absolute sensitivities@FPR$1\%$ depend on the
fraction of off-rhythm mail, which is why calibration on the real Enron is needed.

\noindent\textbf{Invariant~I, verdict.} Multiplex fusion of domain-knowledge layers beats the single graph and
eliminates new-sender false alarms; the advantage is causal, vanishing after shuffling, and complementary to
Invariant~II.

\subsection{Invariant~II: temporal cascade dynamics}
\label{sec:res-inv2}
This subsection tests sub-thesis~T2 (RQ2): whether a compromised account that passes every static layer can be
caught from the cascade it produces, and whether the signal survives a stealthy attacker where volume detection
collapses.

\paragraph{Detector hierarchy.} A compromised account passes every static layer, so the multiplex cannot see it
(AUC $0.49$) and the signal must lie in the dynamics. The detectors form a clear hierarchy on this task
(Tab.~\ref{tab:detectors}, Fig.~\ref{fig:detectors}): local 1-hop features are near-useless (AUC $0.70$,
sensitivity@FPR$1\%\!\approx\!0$), a hand-crafted multi-hop context reaches $0.87$, a static GNN sees the
topology but not time ($0.77$), and the temporal GNN leads (AUC $0.90$, sensitivity@FPR$1\%$ $0.139$). The advantage is causally temporal: after a time permutation the temporal GNN drops
to the static level ($0.90\!\to\!0.72$, sensitivity $0.139\!\to\!0.027$). Robustness to architecture: TGN-lite and TGAT-lite beat the hand-crafted
context in each of the $5$~seeds; the library TGN \citep{rossi2020temporal} matches it on the
operational metric --- the advantage is a property of the \emph{paradigm}, not of a specific model.

\paragraph{The stealthy attacker breaks volume, but not dynamics.} Against an attacker who evades detection by
spreading sends out ($K$~carriers, one per interval), the volume detector breaks down (AUC $0.72\!\to\!0.44$ at
$K{=}1$, below random) because the sending burst disappears, while the temporal GNN holds (AUC $0.60$,
sensitivity@FPR$1\%$ $0.039$ vs $0.017$) because it reads the receiving side of the cascade
(Tab.~\ref{tab:stealth}, Fig.~\ref{fig:stealth}). Against the path-based baseline (Hopper, Tab.~\ref{tab:hopper})
the path-based detector wins under extreme stealth but degrades with reach, and the temporal GNN is the only
detector with no breakdown regime ($0.65$--$0.82$). One blind spot remains (Tab.~\ref{tab:blindspot}): a surgical
attack on a single recipient ($p_\mathrm{inf}{=}0$, fan-out~$1$) is invisible to all detectors (AUC $\sim\!0.46$),
and the signal requires fan-out~$\ge2$, a measured boundary of the threat model.

\paragraph{Stability and inductive generalization.} The cascade model sweep (Tab.~\ref{tab:pinf}) shows the
hierarchy holding across the whole range $p_\mathrm{inf}\!\in\![0.05,0.9]$ and depth; the receiving-side signal
does not vanish at a realistically low $p_\mathrm{inf}$ (temporal GNN $0.91$--$0.96$). Under inductive validation
across disjoint organizations (Tab.~\ref{tab:scale}, Fig.~\ref{fig:scale}), the operational advantage holds on
unseen companies ($4.4\times$ at $19$~companies, $1.4\times$ at $200$) but shrinks with scale: the temporal GNN
is a better operational detector, not a better ranker.

\subsection{Organization susceptibility measurement}
\label{sec:susceptibility}
The third contribution uses these detectors as a measuring instrument. We define group susceptibility as the
residual margin of the fusion detector at FPR$=1\%$, $\mathrm{Susc}(\text{group})=1-\mathrm{Recall@FPR1\%}$, the
fraction of attacks that pass (cf.\ Eq.~\ref{eq:susc-model}). We break the result of the unified event model down
by attack channel and victim seniority (Tab.~\ref{tab:susc}, $20$~seeds).

\midrule

  & Executive & $0.038$ & $0.962$ \\

  & Junior                   & $0.035$ & $0.965$ \\
\bottomrule

\caption{Organization susceptibility \emph{measured} by the fusion detector ($20$~seeds, FPR$=1\%$).

\label{tab:susc}

\noindent Three conclusions follow. (1)~Susceptibility is governed by relation, not rank: cold impersonation
leaves $\mathrm{Susc}\!\approx\!0.96$ at every level, because no layer covers a sender from off-graph, giving a
structural floor to the residual $2\%$. (2)~A compromised account is less dangerous ($0.855$ vs $0.962$), since a
known contact off-rhythm is a sharp, low-FP conjunction. (3)~In the lateral channel, rank acts
counterintuitively: executives, the richest in domain-knowledge layers, are best protected ($0.924$), while the
peripheral mid- and junior-level roles are most exposed ($0.838$/$0.840$). What protects is not status but one's
own embedding in the domain-knowledge graph. The implication runs opposite to the VIP-first program: under a
lateral threat, the largest margin lies on the periphery.

\subsection{Validation on real communication graphs}
\label{sec:res-realgraphs}
We validate the mechanisms on real graphs (Enron, two SNAP graphs), where the structure and rhythms are real and
the attack labels are controlled injections under a matched design: legitimate traffic and attack are edges with
the same distribution of endpoints and degrees, distinguished only by their temporal pattern.

\paragraph{Invariant~I on the real Enron multiplex.} From the headers we build a real structural-temporal
multiplex (contacts, co-recipients, domain, rhythm), keeping OSINT synthetic as a deliberate split. On
$818$~senders and $\sim\!170$k~events, the full multiplex reaches AUC $0.94$ (legitimate traffic on-rhythm)
versus $0.75$ for contacts alone. The off-hours-traffic audit (Fig.~\ref{fig:enronaudit}) shows that the
operational sensitivity was inflated: a realistic figure is $\sim\!0.49$ rather than $0.87$, but the multiplex
advantage over the temporal layer alone rises with the off-hours fraction. Under the sharpest control, a temporal
split of rhythm estimation (rhythm from the early $60\%$, evaluation on the later $40\%$) together with off-hours
traffic (Tab.~\ref{tab:enronsplit}), the temporal layer alone collapses operationally
(Recall@FPR$1\%\!\to\!0$) while the full multiplex stays robust (AUC $0.84$, Recall $0.51$), and its advantage
over time rises once the leakage is removed. The structural signal is real.

\subsection{Rigor: what it means for a detector to work}
\label{sec:res-rigor}
This subsection holds the results to the definition of effectiveness (Sec.~\ref{sec:effectiveness}) and marks the
operational limits, so that the headline numbers are read as a lower bound rather than a deployment claim.

\paragraph{The whole curve and significance.} Instead of a single threshold we report the sensitivity--FPR curve
(Fig.~\ref{fig:fpr}) and a paired test. At a realistically low FPR ($0.1\%$) all methods are weak, which we state
plainly. Hardening the main contrast on~$20$~seeds at the stealthy point (Tab.~\ref{tab:headline-ci}), the
receiving-side signal beats the volume detector significantly and with a large effect (temporal GNN $+0.081$ AUC,
$p<0.001$, Cliff~$\delta{=}0.88$), whereas the advantage of the learned GNN over the hand-crafted context is
operating-point dependent and insignificant in key regimes. The credible claim is narrow: under a stealthy attack the
receiving-side signal, whether learned or hand-crafted, significantly surpasses volume detection, which breaks
down. The learned GNN does not beat everything.

\paragraph{Base rate: why this is a lower bound.} Sensitivity at a given FPR is independent of prevalence, but
precision drops when the attack is rare. From Bayes' theorem $\mathrm{prec}(b)=rb/(rb+\alpha(1-b))$
(\ref{eq:baserate}): at the operating point of sensitivity $0.14$ at FPR$1\%$ and a real prevalence of
$1{:}10^4$, each detected attack comes with $\sim\!700$ false alarms (Tab.~\ref{tab:baserate}), about three orders
of magnitude above the threshold of \citet{ho2019detecting} ($<\!4$/million). The consequence is that at a real
prevalence our detectors, and any detector with a similar ROC curve, are a triage and ranking tool rather than an
autonomous defense; the contribution lies in the signal, the mechanism, and the methodology.
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{prec}(b) \;=\; \frac{r\,b}{r\,b + \alpha\,(1-b)}.
\label{eq:baserate}
\end{equation}

\noindent\textbf{Definition of effectiveness.} A method works operationally if its advantage is causally
relational-temporal (vanishing under shuffle controls), transferable across topologies (the predictivity probe),
and measured at low FPR with explicit prevalence arithmetic. Under this definition, $98\%$ on a mass benchmark
does not attest to detecting spear phishing; what counts is sensitivity to the residual $2\%$, with a stated floor
(cold impersonation) and an explicit threshold.
