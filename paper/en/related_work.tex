\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

We organize the review around the chain of our thesis: \emph{spear phishing as a violation of domain
knowledge} (\ref{sec:rw-spear}), \emph{human and organizational susceptibility} (\ref{sec:rw-suscept}),
\emph{a graph signal instead of content} (\ref{sec:rw-graph}), and \emph{evaluation rigor} (\ref{sec:rw-eval}).
We conclude with a synthesis of the gaps (\ref{sec:rw-gap}).

\subsection{Spear phishing and BEC: why content is insufficient}
\label{sec:rw-spear}
Targeted attacks --- spear phishing, whaling, Business Email Compromise --- differ from mass phishing through
\emph{personalization and context}; a recent survey in \emph{Computers \& Security} systematizes the techniques
and gaps of this domain \citep{birthriya2025detection}. \citet{ho2019detecting} characterized \emph{lateral
phishing} from compromised accounts at a scale of $113$~million emails, establishing an operational low-FPR
threshold ($<\!4$~FP/million); \citet{cidon2019high} targeted ``payload-free'' BEC in production
\citep{cidon2019high}, while BEC surveys describe its evolution and cost \citep{atlam2023business}. The common
denominator of effective methods is a \emph{departure from content}: per-sender behavioral profiles
\citep{gascon2018reading,stringhini2015thataint}, header/stylometry models \citep{duman2016emailprofiler}, and
analysis of relay infrastructure \citep{luo2024characterizing} catch mail from compromised legitimate accounts,
whose content is indistinguishable from normal. This confirms the thesis: maliciousness is determined by
\emph{who} and \emph{in what context}, not by \emph{what} was written.

\subsection{The knowledge-side attack: OSINT and language models}
\label{sec:rw-attack}
The credibility of spear phishing derives from \emph{domain knowledge} built from open-source intelligence (OSINT).
Automated harvesting of traces for spear phishing was studied earlier \citep{edwards2017panning}.
\citet{dewan2014analyzing} showed at the same time that raw social/OSINT features added \emph{little} beyond
content and URLs \citep{dewan2014analyzing,dewan2017facebook} --- a caution we take seriously:
OSINT helps as a \emph{relation constructor} (a shared list, an affiliation), not as a flat feature. Language
models drastically lower the cost of personalization: automated spear phishing campaigns achieve effectiveness
comparable to human experts in human-subject studies \citep{bethany2024evaluating}, and generating realistic
OSINT traces without human-subject studies is possible synthetically \citep{brown2025web}. This shifts the
battleground from \emph{style} to \emph{structure}: since content can be generated perfectly, the defense must
read relations.

\subsection{Human and organizational susceptibility}
\label{sec:rw-suscept}
The human factor explains why the residual $2\%$ is so effective. Susceptibility to spear phishing depends on
personality traits and context \citep{halevi2015spearphishing}, and time pressure together with persuasion cues
(authority, urgency) reduce detectability \citep{butavicius2022why}. Awareness, decision style, and knowledge
moderate detection ability \citep{sturman2025security}. The implication for technical defense is significant:
since susceptibility is a function of \emph{role, relation, and context}, then (a)~a content filter is
insufficient, and (b)~it can be \emph{measured} structurally --- which we do via an organizational
susceptibility metric (Section~\ref{sec:susceptibility}).

\subsection{The graph signal: from GNN foundations to knowledge graphs}
\label{sec:rw-graph}
Our two invariants rest on a mature apparatus of graph learning; we organize it into six threads, indicating
what we borrow and what is missing for a \emph{domain knowledge graph} of email.

\paragraph{Foundations and inductive learning.} Modern graph networks learn node representations through
neighborhood aggregation: the \emph{inductive} GraphSAGE \citep{hamilton2017inductive} (generalization to nodes
unseen during training) and the attention-based GAT \citep{velickovic2018graph} establish the paradigm, of which
our \emph{structural} (rather than identity-based) node features are a deliberately lightweight variant in order
to preserve inductivity \citep{xu2020inductive,hao2020inductive}. Link-prediction and scalable-embedding tasks
\citep{zhang2018link,zhang2018scalable} provide heads for classifying $(s\!\to\!v)$ events.

\paragraph{Multilayer and heterogeneous networks.} The formalism for fusing multiple relations over \emph{one}
set of accounts is the \emph{multiplex} network
\citep{kivela2014multilayer,dedomenico2013mathematical,magnani2013combinatorial,jeude2019multilayer}.
Heterogeneous GNNs learn to fuse relation types: HAN \citep{wang2019heterogeneous}, HGT \citep{hu2020heterogeneous},
MAGNN \citep{fu2020magnn}, and attributed-network embedding \citep{christophides2021overview,cen2019representation}.
This provides the machinery for joining layers, yet \emph{cross-layer (in)consistency as a security signal}
remains unoperationalized for corporate email.

\paragraph{Temporal dynamics.} The second invariant requires a model of time. The memory-based TGN
\citep{rossi2020temporal}, the attention-based DySAT \citep{sankar2020dysat}, EvolveGCN \citep{pareja2020evolvegcn},
ROLAND \citep{you2022roland}, and streaming and spatio-temporal GNNs
\citep{yu2023streaming,wang2022tsgn,nayebi2025spatiotemporal,behrouz2022anomaly} capture graph evolution. Our
TGN-lite is a minimal realization of this paradigm --- deliberately so, in order that the time-shuffle control
isolates the \emph{mechanism} rather than the capacity of the architecture.

\paragraph{Fraud and anomaly detection on graphs.} The closest apparatus comes from fraud detection robust to
\emph{camouflage} and \emph{class imbalance}: CARE-GNN \citep{dou2020enhancing}, GraphConsis \citep{liu2020alleviating},
PC-GNN \citep{liu2021pick}, H2-FDetector \citep{shi2022h2fdetector}, GraphSMOTE \citep{zhao2021graphsmote},
distribution-shift correction \citep{gao2023alleviating,tang2022rethinking}, and the detection of money laundering
and transactional fraud \citep{weber2019antimoney,li2022ttagn,rayana2015collective}. The broader graph-anomaly
literature \citep{ding2019deep,park2020unsupervised,liu2021anomaly,ma2021comprehensive,zhang2021mcgc} ---
including the multiplex setting \citep{li2025umgad} --- as well as predicting user dynamics and roles
\citep{kumar2019predicting,wu2020who,li2022pdgnn,mcauley2015image} provides techniques, yet operates on graphs of
\emph{transactions/reviews}, not \emph{communication with domain knowledge}.

\paragraph{Adversarial robustness and self-supervised learning.} Since an attacker can \emph{fabricate} edges
(our fabrication sweep), the literature on graph structure attacks
\citep{zugner2018adversarial,zugner2019adversarial,dai2022towards} and defenses
\citep{zhang2020gnnguard,jin2020graph,cai2021structural,wang2023label} is relevant. Contrastive and
self-supervised learning \citep{hassani2020contrastive,wang2021selfsupervised,singh2024node,yan2024topological}
offers a label-free signal --- relevant given the absence of real BEC labels.

\paragraph{Graphs in security: phishing and threat intelligence.} Graph-based phishing detection has so far
targeted mainly \emph{web pages} and \emph{transactions}: graph-theoretic and visual approaches for web pages
\citep{tan2020graph,lin2021phishpedia,abdelnabi2020visualphishnet}, attention over the email graph
\citep{safran2025phishinggnn}, edge-feature GraphSAGE \citep{lo2021egraphsage}, propagation over URL--IP graphs
\citep{guo2025efficient,li2022pdgnn}, chain/blockchain-based detection
\citep{daluwatta2022cgraph,edirimannage2022phishchain}, and the detection of phishing and accounts in social
media \citep{aggarwal2013phishari,shu2017user,koide2024chatspamdetector,song2025insight,dube2024building}. In
parallel, cyber threat intelligence (CTI) knowledge graphs structure intelligence
\citep{cheng2024ctinexus,fieblinger2024actionable,li2021siege}. \emph{Cross-view (in)consistency} as an anomaly
signal is known \citep{liu2020alleviating}, but --- to our knowledge --- not operationalized as a \emph{domain
knowledge graph} of corporate email for security. This is the gap we fill.

\subsection{Evaluation rigor: pitfalls and leak control}
\label{sec:rw-eval}
A central caution is that ML detectors in security are easily \emph{inflated} by leaks, unfair splitting, and
headline AUC instead of operational metrics \citep{arp2022dos}; time-aware evaluation (TESSERACT) shows how
temporal leakage distorts results \citep{pendlebury2019tesseract}. These works motivate our \emph{leak-aware}
protocol and \emph{predictivity probe}: we ask not only ``how high is the AUC'' but ``whether the result
transfers beyond a single corpus and whether it is an artifact of the generator''. By this same measure we
define the notion of a method's effectiveness.

\subsection{Gaps and positioning of the work}
\label{sec:rw-gap}
Three gaps emerge from the review. \emph{First}, spear phishing detection remains dominated by the content
signal, whereas the residual $2\%$ bypasses it by definition --- there is no \emph{unified} relational treatment
for lateral/BEC and external impersonation. \emph{Second}, graph approaches target the web/transactions, not the
\emph{domain knowledge graph} of communication, and organizational susceptibility is not \emph{measured}
structurally. \emph{Third}, evaluations rarely control for leaks and transfer across topologies, so
``effectiveness'' tends to be ill-defined. The present work addresses all three: with the notion of a domain
knowledge graph, its two detection invariants, a susceptibility metric, and a leak-aware methodology that
defines effectiveness.
