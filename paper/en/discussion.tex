\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

The two invariants address disjoint failure modes, and the susceptibility measure reveals where the remaining
margin lies. We focus here on the mechanism's implications and its operational limits.

\subsection{Interpretation: two invariants, two failure modes}
The two invariants are complementary responses to two disjoint modes of targeted attack
(Sec.~\ref{sec:res-inv1},~\ref{sec:res-inv2}). Per-threat ablation makes this concrete: for
new senders, the signal is carried by OSINT while time is useless; for compromises, the reverse holds. This explains
why single-signal approaches fail on the targeted tail. Whether it is a content filter, a contact graph, or a volumetric detector,
each is blind to at least one of these modes.

A second result concerns the stealthy attacker. A volumetric detector in the spirit of COMPA
\citep{stringhini2015thataint} assumes that a compromise shows up as a surge; when the attacker deliberately
spreads the sends over time, the volumetric signal vanishes and its AUC drops below the random baseline. Cascade dynamics instead
responds to the recipient side, the wave-like signature among the neighbors, which the sender cannot hide. This agrees
with Ho et al.\ \citep{ho2019detecting} that lateral phishing needs a signal beyond the content of a single
message; we differ in locating that signal in the dynamics of the domain knowledge graph rather than in headers
or relays.

\subsection{Practical implications}
The central finding from Sec.~\ref{sec:susceptibility} is that the periphery, not the executives, is most exposed under a lateral threat. For the lateral channel, the largest unaddressed margin lies on the periphery, specifically the mid- and junior-level roles. This suggests that awareness programs and hard controls, such as out-of-band verification for transfers, should cover the periphery rather than focusing solely on the top. Detection resources are similarly best directed where the domain
knowledge graph is sparse, as that is where the residual margin is largest. For the cold impersonation
channel, however, no relational layer suffices ($\mathrm{Susc}\!\approx\!0.96$ regardless of rank). Here the graph must
be combined with content and process controls, as the fusion experiment (Sec.~\ref{sec:res-content}) confirms.
The domain knowledge graph is thus a prioritization tool, not a standalone product; it identifies
who is most weakly protected and through which relationship.

The result also bears on the human factor. Human susceptibility rises under time pressure and authority cues
while awareness training moderates it only partially (Section~\ref{sec:related}), so a structural susceptibility
measure allows limited training interventions to target the roles the technology protects least.

\subsection{Limitations}
The limitations define the scope of these claims. First, the maliciousness is synthetic. No public corpus
links real OSINT facts with labeled compromises, so we generate attack labels on real structure (Enron, SNAP) or
on a synthetic population of twins. We validate the mechanism (out-of-rhythm mail separates
compromises; layer inconsistency separates impersonation), not the detection of real intrusions. The OSINT
multiplex advantage of Invariant~I remains purely synthetic; it is a proof of soundness rather than a
measurement on real traffic. There is also some circularity: since the synthetic population is generated graph-first,
detecting a violation of that structure is partly built in. This is reflected in the layer analysis
(\ref{sec:res-inv1}), which shows the temporal layer carrying almost all of Invariant~I's signal. Structural
and OSINT layers contribute little unless the topology is artificially decorrelated. The dominant signal of
Invariant~I thus reduces to rhythm consistency detecting off-rhythm mail, while the cascade result
(Invariant~II) establishes the receiving-side signal much more robustly on real graphs. Second, the
susceptibility measurement inherits these assumptions. The $\mathrm{Susc}$ figures are relative to our event
model and population structure (19~companies of about 8 people, one domain per company). While the direction of the
conclusions (relationship over rank; periphery most exposed) is robust, the exact values do not
transfer directly to every organization. This is partly definitional: executives are layer-rich by
construction, so their stronger structural protection follows from how we tied layer richness to
seniority. This coupling should be stress-tested under alternative assignments before generalizing the periphery-first
recommendation. Third, the scale is limited ($N{=}150$ twins; real graphs up to about 2k
nodes), making the comparison with heavy GNN architectures (GATNE/HGT) a sanity check rather than a full benchmark.
Finally, the operational threshold remains a gap. Even the best operating point (sensitivity@FPR$1\%$) is orders of
magnitude from the deployment threshold on real mail (Ho et al.\ \citep{ho2019detecting}: $<\!4$~FP/M); we report
it as a requirement we are beginning to meet rather than a record.

\subsection{Future work directions}
The most important next step is validation on a corpus with labeled, real compromises, turning the proof of
mechanism into a measurement of detectability. On the modeling side, the hand-crafted consistency features could
give way to a learned multiplex GNN with explicit cross-layer consistency regularization, validated inductively
on unseen organizations. The threat model itself should then be stressed: an attacker that \emph{learns} the
domain knowledge graph (LLM~$+$~OSINT \citep{bethany2024evaluating}) and tunes rhythm and relationships, together
with population drift over time (TESSERACT \citep{pendlebury2019tesseract}). Finally, the measured $\mathrm{Susc}$
is a natural steering signal for dynamic prioritization of controls and personalization of training, provided an
evaluation ties it to a real reduction in click-through.
