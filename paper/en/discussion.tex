\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

The results converge on one thesis: residual spear phishing is detectable to the degree that it violates the
victim's observable domain knowledge, and that degree can be measured. We interpret the mechanism, outline the
practical implications, and state the limits.

\subsection{Interpretation: two invariants, two failure modes}
The two invariants are complementary responses to two disjoint modes of targeted attack. Cross-layer
inconsistency (\ref{sec:res-inv1}) addresses the new sender: role or authority impersonation is known in one
layer, such as a name or domain, but inconsistent in others, with no OSINT trace and no relationship history,
which breaks the pattern of a genuine relationship. Cascade dynamics (\ref{sec:res-inv2}) addresses the
compromised account, which is consistent across all static layers because it is a genuine contact, yet betrays
itself in time by sending out of rhythm and creating a recipient-side cascade. The per-threat ablation makes the
complementarity concrete: for new senders the signal is carried by OSINT and time is useless, and for compromises
it is the reverse. This is why single approaches fail on the residual $2\%$ (a content filter, a contact graph,
or a volumetric detector on its own): each is blind to one of the two modes.

A second result concerns the stealthy attacker. A volumetric detector in the spirit of COMPA
\citep{stringhini2015thataint} assumes that a compromise shows up as a surge; when the attacker deliberately
spreads the sending out, the volumetric signal vanishes and its AUC falls below random. Cascade dynamics instead
reads the recipient side, the wave-like signature among the neighbors, which the sender cannot hide. This agrees
with Ho et al.\ \citep{ho2019detecting} that lateral phishing needs a signal beyond the content of a single
message; we differ in locating that signal in the dynamics of the domain knowledge graph rather than in headers
or relays.

\subsection{Practical implications}
One implication of the susceptibility measurement (\ref{sec:susceptibility}) runs counter to common practice.
Typical defense programs treat executives as the most exposed. Our measurement shows that for the lateral channel
the opposite holds: executives, densely embedded in the domain knowledge graph, are best protected by the
structural signal alone, while the largest unaddressed margin lies on the periphery, the mid- and junior-level
roles. The recommendation is two-part. First, awareness programs and hard controls, such as out-of-band
verification for transfers, should cover the periphery and not only the top. Second, detection resources are
worth directing where the domain knowledge graph is sparse, since that is where the residual margin is largest.
For the cold impersonation channel the message is different: no relational layer suffices
($\mathrm{Susc}\!\approx\!0.96$ regardless of rank), so here the graph must be combined with content and process
controls, as the fusion experiment confirms. The domain knowledge graph is therefore a prioritization layer
rather than a standalone product: it indicates who, and through which relationship, is most weakly protected.

The result also bears on the human factor. Human susceptibility rises under time pressure and authority cues
while awareness training moderates it only partially (Section~\ref{sec:related}), so a structural susceptibility
measure lets limited training interventions be aimed at the roles the technology protects most weakly.

\subsection{Limitations}
The limitations define the scope of the claims. \textit{First, maliciousness is synthetic.} No public corpus
links real OSINT facts with labeled compromises, so we generate attack labels on real structure (Enron, SNAP) or
on a synthetic population of twins. We therefore validate the mechanism (out-of-rhythm mail separates
compromises; layer inconsistency separates impersonation), not the detection of real intrusions. The OSINT
multiplex advantage of Invariant~I remains synthetic only, and we read it as a proof of soundness rather than a
measurement on real traffic. There is also some circularity: the synthetic population is generated graph-first,
so detecting a violation of that structure is partly built in. In line with this, the layer analysis
(\ref{sec:res-inv1}) shows the temporal layer carrying almost all of Invariant~I's signal, with the structural
and OSINT layers contributing little unless the topology is artificially decorrelated; the headline of
Invariant~I then reduces to rhythm consistency detecting off-rhythm mail, whereas the cascade result
(Invariant~II) establishes the receiving-side signal much more robustly on real graphs. \textit{Second, the
susceptibility measurement inherits these assumptions.} The $\mathrm{Susc}$ figures are relative to our event
model and population structure (19~companies of about 8 people, one domain per company); the direction of the
conclusions (relationship over rank; periphery most exposed) is robust, but the exact values do not
transfer directly to every organization. The direction is also partly definitional: executives are layer-rich by
construction, so their stronger structural protection follows in part from how we tied layer richness to
seniority, a coupling that should be stress-tested under alternative assignments before the periphery-first
recommendation is generalized. \textit{Third, the scale is limited} ($N{=}150$ twins; real graphs up to about 2k
nodes), so the comparison with heavy GNN architectures (GATNE/HGT) is a sanity check, not a full benchmark.
\textit{Fourth, the operational threshold.} Even the best operating point (sensitivity@FPR$1\%$) is orders of
magnitude from the deployment threshold on real mail (Ho et al.\ \citep{ho2019detecting}: $<\!4$~FP/M); we report
it as a requirement we are starting to meet, not a record.

\subsection{Future work directions}
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep,topsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Real BEC labels.} The most important next step is validation on a corpus
  with labeled, real compromises --- to turn the proof of mechanism into a measurement of detectability.
  \item \textbf{A learned, inductive domain knowledge graph.} Replacing the hand-crafted consistency features
  with a learned multiplex GNN with explicit cross-layer consistency regularization, validated
  inductively on unseen organizations.
  \item \textbf{Adaptive attack and drift.} Studying an attacker that \emph{learns}
  the domain knowledge graph (LLM~$+$~OSINT \citep{bethany2024evaluating}) and tunes rhythm/relationships, as well as population drift
  over time (TESSERACT \citep{pendlebury2019tesseract}).
  \item \textbf{Susceptibility as a steering signal.} Using the measured $\mathrm{Susc}$ for
  dynamic prioritization of controls and personalization of training --- with an evaluation of whether it lowers the real
  click-through rate.
\end{itemize}
